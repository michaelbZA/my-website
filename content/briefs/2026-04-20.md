Good morning. The dominant story this week is the Iran-US confrontation, which has moved from diplomatic tension to direct military action. US Marines boarded and seized an Iranian-flagged cargo vessel near the Strait of Hormuz over the weekend, and Iran has now confirmed it won't send negotiators to the planned Pakistan talks, effectively killing the most credible near-term route to a ceasefire. Oil has rebounded on the news. With Iran threatening retaliation and the Islamabad channel now in doubt, this is the most acute escalation risk in months.

**Mandelson/Starmer.** Starmer faces a Commons statement today on how Peter Mandelson was appointed US ambassador despite failing security vetting — with the Foreign Office having overruled that failure without disclosure. The FT reports that sacked Foreign Office permanent secretary Olly Robbins is taking legal advice and will give a "full account" to MPs on Tuesday. Starmer's allies are openly worried this could be existential for his leadership. Watch Tuesday's testimony closely.

**BBC insider trading investigation.** The BBC says it has identified a pattern of unusual trading spikes ahead of Trump's public announcements — tariff pauses, policy reversals and the like. This isn't a new allegation but the BBC presenting it as a documented pattern rather than speculation gives it more weight. Expect this to run.

**Chinese satellites filling the intelligence gap over Iran.** The Economist reports that as US imagery went dark over the conflict zone, Chinese commercial satellites have been providing Iran with usable reconnaissance data. That's a meaningful shift in the information asymmetry that Western forces have historically relied on, and it has broader implications for any future theatre where China chooses to play that card.

**FTSE executive pay.** FT reports FTSE bosses have received an average 18% pay increase this year, with boards simultaneously stripping ESG metrics out of remuneration packages. The combination — higher pay, fewer accountability strings — will land badly in the current political climate and is likely to draw a response from the government.

**Blue Origin setback.** New Glenn's third launch successfully recovered its first stage but deposited a customer satellite in the wrong orbit. A meaningful blow to Blue Origin's credibility as it competes for NASA and DoD contracts, and it complicates the Moon programme timeline.

**Watch today and tomorrow:** Starmer's Commons statement this afternoon, then Olly Robbins before MPs on Tuesday — that's where the real damage, if any, gets done.
