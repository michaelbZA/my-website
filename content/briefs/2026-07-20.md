Andy Burnham takes over as Prime Minister today, delivering his first Downing Street speech this morning. The transition is faster than many expected given Starmer only won the 2024 election two years ago. The immediate policy question for markets is energy. Burnham's deputy Lucy Powell was on the BBC yesterday trying to contain speculation that he would reopen North Sea licensing, insisting the manifesto commitment against new exploration licences stands — but her language about a "change of emphasis" and a "more pragmatic approach" with industry suggests the door is not fully closed. Watch what Burnham actually says today rather than what his allies said yesterday.

The fiscal backdrop he inherits is tight, and the FT's commentary this morning is blunt: the biggest risk for the new PM is convincing himself there is a free lunch available. Burnham built his Manchester reputation on delivery within constraints; whether that translates to Whitehall is the open question.

Oil hit $90 a barrel overnight after Iran struck tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, with two ships reportedly exploding. Prices pulled back after Tehran indicated it had received ceasefire proposals from mediators. Rubio said the US remains open to diplomacy after nine consecutive nights of American strikes on Iran. The situation is live and the spread between the spike high and current prices tells you the market is treating this as de-escalatory for now — but the Hormuz risk premium is not going away while strikes continue.

Morgan Stanley has positioned itself as the lead arranger for data centre financing structures tied to the AI build-out, according to the FT. This is worth noting for anyone thinking about infrastructure credit exposure — the bank is effectively warehousing and syndicating the debt behind hyperscaler capex, which is a different risk profile to vanilla corporate lending.

Jensen Huang's Tokyo visit concluded with deals across Japan's tech ecosystem, per TechCrunch. No single headline number, but the direction is consistent with Nvidia cementing sovereign AI partnerships ahead of any potential US export restriction tightening.

Netflix has paid $587 million in cash for InterPositive, Ben Affleck's AI filmmaking startup. The price is large enough to signal that Netflix views AI-native content production as a near-term competitive necessity rather than an experiment.

Burnham's first Commons statement is expected later this week once he has formally kissed hands and assembled a cabinet. The more immediate scheduled event: Bank of England Deputy Governor Sarah Breeden speaks tomorrow on the financial stability outlook — her first public remarks since the latest CPI print, and worth watching for any shift in tone on the rate path.
